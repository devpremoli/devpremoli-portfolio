Six years into my software engineering career, I want to share some of the lessons I've picked up building distributed systems across very different industries — finance, healthcare, and telecom.

## Starting in Finance

My first major engineering role was at HSBC, working on transaction processing systems. Banking taught me that **correctness is non-negotiable**. When you're processing millions of dollars in settlements, "eventually consistent" isn't good enough.

I learned to think deeply about:

- **Transaction isolation levels** — what happens when two operations touch the same account?
- **Idempotency** — if a payment request is retried, it should not be charged twice
- **Audit trails** — every state change must be traceable

Spring Batch became my workhorse for high-volume batch processing. We'd process millions of transactions nightly, and the framework's restart, skip, and retry capabilities were essential.

## Scaling in Healthcare

At Cardinal Health, the challenge shifted from financial correctness to **data interoperability**. Healthcare systems speak HL7 and FHIR — standards that are powerful but complex.

The key lesson here was building reliable integrations between systems that don't natively talk to each other. Event-driven patterns with AWS Lambda, S3, and SNS/SQS became the glue.

Healthcare also reinforced the importance of **security and compliance**. HIPAA isn't just a checkbox — it fundamentally shapes how you design data flows, access controls, and audit logging.

## Investing in Quality at Fidelity

At Fidelity Investments, I worked on portfolio valuation systems that needed to calculate real-time positions across thousands of accounts. The challenge was **performance under load**.

During high market-volume windows, our services needed to process massive parallel calculations. Java's `CompletableFuture` and careful thread pool management were critical:

```java
List<CompletableFuture<Valuation>> futures = accounts.stream()
    .map(account -> CompletableFuture.supplyAsync(
        () -> calculateValuation(account),
        executorService
    ))
    .toList();

CompletableFuture.allOf(futures.toArray(new CompletableFuture[0])).join();
```

This is where I also started working with Python and FastAPI for ML-adjacent services — fraud risk scoring models that needed low-latency inference endpoints.

## Telecom and the Scale Problem

Comcast was a different beast. Subscriber-scale means millions of users, and the systems need to handle that volume with low latency. This is where I deepened my experience with:

- **gRPC for inter-service communication** — binary protocol, strongly typed, much faster than REST for internal services
- **GraphQL for frontend APIs** — let the UI request exactly what it needs
- **Spring Cloud microservices** — service discovery, config server, API gateway
- **ETL at scale** — AWS Glue + PySpark for processing terabytes of subscriber data

The biggest lesson from telecom: **observability is not optional at scale**. You can't debug a distributed system with print statements. Distributed tracing, structured logging, and metrics are table stakes.

## What I've Learned Along the Way

Looking back, a few principles have served me well across every domain:

1. **Understand the domain before writing code** — the best technical solution is useless if it doesn't solve the actual business problem
2. **Design for failure** — distributed systems fail in creative ways. Circuit breakers, retries with backoff, dead letter queues, and graceful degradation aren't premature optimization — they're requirements
3. **Keep it simple until you can't** — don't reach for Kafka when a database queue will do. Don't build microservices when a well-structured monolith would work. Complexity has a cost
4. **Invest in testing** — integration tests that hit real databases and message brokers catch bugs that unit tests with mocks never will
5. **Stay curious** — the field moves fast. LLMs, RAG pipelines, and vector databases weren't in my toolkit two years ago. Now they're central to my work at Paycom

---

Every industry teaches you something different. Finance teaches correctness. Healthcare teaches interoperability. Telecom teaches scale. The common thread is building systems that are reliable, maintainable, and designed to evolve.

I'm excited about what comes next — especially at the intersection of AI and enterprise engineering. The tools are getting more powerful, and the problems are getting more interesting.
